<Intuition>
Module 6 ended on a cliffhanger: when "next" means *most important* rather
than *oldest*, you need a priority queue — and serving from a sorted list
costs O(n) per insert. The heap's insight is that **total order is
overkill.** To serve the minimum, you only need to know the minimum; the
rest can stay comfortably vague. So the heap maintains one lazy promise —
every parent ≤ its children — which pins the extreme element to the root
while leaving siblings in cheerful disorder. Less order maintained = less
work per operation: O(log n) instead of O(n).
</Intuition>

<Concept>
## The duality: a tree with no pointers

A heap is a *complete* binary tree — every level full, last level packed
left. Completeness means the tree can be flattened into an array with
zero pointers: **children of index i sit at 2i+1 and 2i+2; the parent at
(i−1)//2.** The index math *is* the edges.

This is the lesson the visual insists on by showing both views at once:
"swap with parent" on the tree and "swap slots 4 and 1" in the array are
the same event. No allocation, no pointer chasing, perfect locality —
the heap is the rare structure that's both asymptotically and
constant-factor fast.

Two repair moves maintain the invariant, both walking one root-to-leaf
path (height = ⌊log₂ n⌋, thanks to completeness):

- **sift up** — new element appended at the end bubbles up while smaller
  than its parent. Powers `push`.
- **sift down** — root replaced by the last element sinks, swapping with
  its *smaller* child. Powers `pop-min`.
</Concept>

<Concept>
## heapify: the O(n) that sounds illegal

Building a heap from n arbitrary items by pushing one at a time costs
O(n log n). `heapify` does it in **O(n)** by running sift_down bottom-up
from the last internal node. The accounting: sift_down's cost is the
node's *height*, and heights are wildly lopsided in a complete tree —
half the nodes are leaves (height 0, free), a quarter have height 1, an
eighth height 2… The sum Σ n/2^(h+1) · h converges to O(n). Pushing
one-by-one charges every element log-of-size; bottom-up charges by
height, and heights are mostly zero. This is a classic interview
flashpoint precisely because the true answer sounds like a mistake.
</Concept>

<RealWorld source="OS scheduler">
Your kernel's CFS scheduler answers "which task runs next?" thousands of
times per second over thousands of tasks — always the one with least
runtime so far, always one pop away. Uber dispatch: nearest idle driver =
pop-min on a heap keyed by ETA. A search ranker's "top 10 results from
50 million candidates" never sorts 50 million — it streams them past a
size-10 heap. Whenever a system serves *best-next* under churn, there is
a heap under the floorboards.
</RealWorld>

<Concept>
## The streaming patterns

**Top-K:** keep a *min*-heap of size k — yes, min, for top-K-largest.
The root is the weakest current champion, i.e. the only one a newcomer
needs to beat. Each of n events: O(1) rejection or O(log k) replacement —
O(n log k) total, O(k) memory, stream never stored.

**Two-heap median:** a max-heap of the lower half and a min-heap of the
upper half, sizes kept within 1. The median is a root read — O(1) query,
O(log n) insert — where a sorted array would pay O(n) per insert. This
pair is the standard answer to "running median over a stream."
</Concept>

<Gotcha>
`heapq` is functions over a plain list, and it is **min-heap only**. For
a max-heap, push negated keys — `heappush(h, -x)` — and negate on the way
out; for compound priorities push tuples `(priority, tiebreak, item)`,
remembering tuples compare element-wise, so equal priorities fall through
to comparing items (crash city for un-comparable objects — hence the
tiebreak counter). And never `heap[1]` expecting the second-smallest:
below the root, a heap is *not* sorted; that vagueness is exactly what
you paid less to get.
</Gotcha>

<Aside title="Heapsort in one paragraph — and why Python doesn't use it">
heapify (O(n)), then repeatedly swap the root to the shrinking suffix and
sift_down the prefix: sorted array, in place, Θ(n log n) worst case,
no recursion. The catch is memory behavior: sift paths jump 2i+1 strides —
cache-hostile — so Timsort's sequential merges beat it in practice
despite identical asymptotics. Heapsort survives as the worst-case
insurance inside C++'s introsort: quicksort until the depth limit, then
heapsort. Module 10 races them.
</Aside>

<Cheatsheet>
The whole structure from the invariant: **parent ≤ children keeps the min
at the root; completeness keeps height at log n and lets the tree live in
an array at 2i+1/2i+2; every operation is one path repair.** heapify is
O(n) — say "heights telescope" and mean it.
</Cheatsheet>
