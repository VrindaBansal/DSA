<Intuition>
Some operations are cheap a thousand times and brutal once. `list.append`
is one memory write… until the array is full, and then it's "allocate a
bigger block and copy *everything*." Judging that operation by its worst
single call is slander; judging it by its typical call is denial.
**Amortized analysis is the honest middle: total cost of the whole sequence,
divided by the number of operations — guaranteed, not averaged over luck.**
It's the accountant's view: the rare big invoice was prepaid by a thousand
small deposits.
</Intuition>

<Concept>
## The dynamic array, precisely

Python's `list` keeps a contiguous block with some spare **capacity**. When
`append` finds no spare slot, it allocates a bigger block (call it 2×,
CPython actually uses ~1.125×), copies all n elements over, then appends.
So an append costs 1 normally — and n at the resize.

Watch the visual: the per-append cost spikes exactly at the doublings, and
the **running average line stays flat**. That flat line is amortized O(1),
drawn rather than claimed. Push elements yourself and try to make the
average misbehave — you can't, and the *why* is the geometric series below.
</Concept>

<Concept>
## The proof in one paragraph

Start at capacity 1 and append n times. Resizes fire at sizes 1, 2, 4, 8,
…, and the resize at size k copies k elements. Total copies:

```
1 + 2 + 4 + ... + n/2 + n  <  2n
```

A geometric series is dominated by its last term — the whole history of
copies costs less than twice the final size. Add the n unavoidable writes:
under 3n total work for n appends, so **O(1) amortized per append**. No
randomness, no "usually": *any* sequence of n appends costs O(n), even one
chosen by an adversary.

The same result via the **banker's trick**: charge every append 3 coins —
one to write itself, two banked. When a resize copies k elements, the k/2
elements appended since the last resize have exactly k coins banked between
them. The invoice was prepaid. If you can invent a coin-charging scheme
that never goes negative, you've proven an amortized bound — that's the
whole method, and it transfers to the two-stack queue in Module 6 verbatim.
</Concept>

<Gotcha>
Growth must be **multiplicative**. Grow by a fixed +16 instead of ×2 and a
resize fires every 16 appends, each copying the whole current array:
16 + 32 + 48 + … ≈ n²/32 total — **O(n) amortized per append**, a quadratic
bulk load hiding behind an innocent-looking constant. If you ever hand-roll
a growable buffer, the growth factor is the entire design.
</Gotcha>

<RealWorld source="Game engine">
A physics engine appends collision events to a dynamic array every frame,
16.6ms budget. Throughput is fine — amortized O(1) promised and delivered.
Then one frame in a thousand lands on the resize: a multi-megabyte copy,
the frame takes 40ms, and the game visibly hitches. Nothing is *wrong* —
amortized analysis answers a throughput question, and the engine asked a
latency one. The fixes are worst-case tools: preallocate
(`reserve`-style), or a ring buffer (Module 6) whose O(1) has no asterisk.
Amortized vs worst-case is not pedantry; it's whether your frame drops.
</RealWorld>

<Aside title="Why CPython grows by ~1.125× instead of 2×">
Any factor r > 1 keeps the series geometric — the amortized argument
survives with a bigger constant (cost ≈ r/(r−1) per element). CPython trades
a larger constant for tighter memory: doubling can strand almost half the
allocation as unused capacity, painful when lists are huge. There's also a
folklore argument about r < φ ≈ 1.618 letting allocators reuse the sum of
previously freed blocks for the next growth. Growth factor choice is a
memory-vs-copies dial, not a correctness question.
</Aside>

<Cheatsheet>
Say it the way an interviewer wants to hear it: **"append is amortized O(1)
— resizes copy 1+2+4+…+n which is a geometric series under 2n, so any n
appends cost O(n) total. If I need worst-case O(1) — real-time paths — I
preallocate or use a ring buffer."** Two sentences, contract and escape
hatch.
</Cheatsheet>
