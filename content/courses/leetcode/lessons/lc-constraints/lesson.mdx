<Intuition>
Most beginners skip straight past the "Constraints" section of a problem. It's
actually a **cheat sheet the problem-setter left for you.** The size of the
input quietly tells you which complexity your solution must hit — and therefore
which family of algorithms is even on the table. A bound of `n ≤ 20` and a
bound of `n ≤ 10⁵` are asking for completely different solutions, and you can
know that *before you have any idea how to solve it.* Learning to read the
number is one of the highest-leverage habits in competitive-style problem
solving.
</Intuition>

<Concept>
## Turn the bound into a budget

Judges (and interviews) roughly allow **about 10⁸ simple operations per
second.** So take the biggest input size, plug it into candidate complexities,
and see what fits inside ~10⁸ (a couple hundred million, to be safe):

| If n is up to… | You can afford | Which means |
|---|---|---|
| 10–12 | O(n!) | permutations, brute over all orderings |
| ≤ 20–25 | O(2ⁿ) | subsets, bitmask DP, backtracking over choices |
| ≤ 500 | O(n³) | triple loops, some 2-D DP with an inner scan |
| ≤ 5,000 | O(n²) | nested loops, most 2-D DP |
| ≤ 10⁵–10⁶ | O(n log n) or O(n) | sort, heap, two pointers, sliding window, hashing |
| ≥ 10⁷–10⁸ | O(n) or O(log n) | a single pass, or binary search |

The move: read n, find the row, and now you know the shape of the answer. If
you *think* of an O(n²) idea but n is 10⁵, don't even write it — it will time
out, and the constraint just told you to keep looking.
</Concept>

<Concept>
## A tiny n is a gift, not a trap

When you see a suspiciously small bound — `n ≤ 18`, `n ≤ 22` — next to language
like "try all combinations," "assign each item," or "visit every subset,"
that's the setter telling you **an exponential solution is intended and will
pass.** 2²⁰ is about a million; 2²⁵ is 33 million — both trivial. So instead of
straining for a polynomial trick that may not exist, reach confidently for
backtracking or bitmask DP. The small number is permission.

The reverse is also true: a *huge* bound (10⁹, or "up to 10¹⁸") usually means
the answer is O(log n) or even O(1) math — you can't even afford to *look* at
every element, so the intended solution must skip most of them (binary search,
a closed-form formula, or number theory).
</Concept>

<Aside title="What about the 10⁸/second number?">
It's a rule of thumb, not a law — real throughput depends on the constant
factor (Python is slower than C++; dictionary ops cost more than integer adds).
For estimating, round hard: treat anything over ~10⁹ total operations as "too
slow," anything under ~10⁷ as "definitely fine," and be thoughtful in between.
The point isn't precision; it's ruling out the double loop before you waste ten
minutes on it.
</Aside>

<Cheatsheet>
Before you code, read n and pick your ceiling: **n ≤ 20 → exponential is fine
(backtracking); n ≤ 5,000 → O(n²) ok; n ≥ 10⁵ → you need O(n log n) or O(n);
n ≥ 10⁸ → O(log n) or math.** Convert the bound to ~10⁸ ops/second and let it
delete whole families of algorithms for you. The constraint is a hint, not
decoration.
</Cheatsheet>
