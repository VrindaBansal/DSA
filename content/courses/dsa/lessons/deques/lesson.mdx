<Intuition>
A queue commits to one direction: in at the back, out at the front. A
**deque** (double-ended queue, say "deck") refuses to choose — both ends are
cheap, both ends are legal. That sounds like a luxury until you meet problems
where the *front* and the *back* mean different things: a sliding window
evicts from the left while admitting on the right; an undo buffer drops its
oldest entry when full while pushing the newest. One structure, two live
ends.
</Intuition>

<Concept>
## Why both ends can be O(1)

CPython's `deque` is a **doubly-linked list of blocks**, each block a small
fixed array of 64 slots. The structure keeps pointers to the leftmost and
rightmost blocks, and each of those blocks keeps spare capacity at its outer
edge. `append` writes into the right block's spare slot; `appendleft` writes
into the left block's; only when a block fills does the deque allocate one
more block and link it in — O(1) either way.

Compare the `list`: one contiguous array, so the back has spare capacity but
the front *is* the memory address where the data starts. Making room at the
front means shifting everything. The deque pays for its cheap ends elsewhere:
indexing `d[i]` must walk the block chain — O(n) in the middle. Every layout
is a bet on which operations you'll call in a loop.
</Concept>

<Check id="q-deque-short" />

<RealWorld source="Print spooler">
The office print spooler is a deque wearing a trench coat. Jobs join at the
back; the printer drains from the front — plain FIFO so far. But "cancel my
job" removes from *wherever*, and "URGENT — reprint the contract" jumps the
line entirely. Real spoolers handle the urgent case by keeping *multiple
queues by priority band* and draining the highest band first — which is a
low-rent priority queue, and exactly where this lesson is headed.
</RealWorld>

<Concept>
## The frontier: when the queue IS the algorithm

Breadth-first search keeps a **frontier** — the set of discovered-but-not-
yet-expanded nodes — in a queue. Pop the front, visit it, push its unseen
neighbors on the back. That's the whole algorithm. The FIFO discipline is
doing all the intellectual work: because nodes are expanded in discovery
order, every node at distance k is processed before any node at distance
k+1. The traversal sweeps outward in perfect rings, which is why BFS finds
shortest paths in unweighted graphs.

Here is the punchline you'll reuse in Module 9: **swap the queue for a stack
and the identical loop becomes DFS.** The container's discipline — not the
loop — decides the traversal. You'll watch the two run side by side on the
same graph there.
</Concept>

<Concept>
## When FIFO stops being fair enough

An emergency room does not treat patients in arrival order; it treats the
most critical first. The moment "next" means **most important** instead of
**oldest**, the queue contract is the wrong contract. You want a **priority
queue**: `push(item, priority)` and `pop_min()` (or max).

Preview of Module 7, so the shape is familiar when you arrive: the standard
implementation is a **binary heap** — a nearly-complete binary tree, stored
flat in an array, holding one invariant (every parent ≤ its children). Push
and pop each cost **O(log n)**: the price of keeping the extreme element
always one step away. Python ships it as `heapq`.

The progression of this module, in one line each:

- `stack` — serve the **newest** (LIFO)
- `queue` — serve the **oldest** (FIFO)
- `deque` — serve **either end**
- `priority queue` — serve the **most important**, O(log n)
</Concept>

<Check id="q-pq-preview" />

<Gotcha>
`deque(maxlen=k)` is wonderful and sneaky: when full, an `append` **silently
evicts** from the far end instead of raising. Perfect for "keep the last k
log lines," an unpleasant surprise if you assumed queue-full was an error.
Bounded ≠ raising — read the contract you're actually getting.
</Gotcha>

<Concept>
## Build it: a queue from two stacks

Classic structural exercise, and the best amortized-analysis rep after
Module 1: make FIFO out of two LIFOs. Push arrivals onto `inbox`; when you
need to dequeue and `outbox` is empty, pour `inbox` into it — the pour
reverses order, so `outbox` pops oldest-first. One unlucky dequeue pays O(n)
for the pour, yet each element is moved at most twice in its lifetime, so
the *sequence* averages O(1) per operation.
</Concept>
