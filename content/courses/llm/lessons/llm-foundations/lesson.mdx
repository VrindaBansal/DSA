<Intuition>
Strip away the product, the chat window, and the hype, and a large language
model is one function: **given some text, predict what token comes next.**
That's it. It doesn't "look things up," it doesn't "run logic," it doesn't
have a plan. It has billions of numbers — *weights* — tuned so that its
next-token guesses match the patterns of an enormous amount of human text.
Everything that amazes you (fluent essays, working code) and everything that
burns you (confident nonsense, stale facts) falls out of that single sentence.
This lesson installs the mental model; every other lesson in this course is a
zoom-in on one part of it.
</Intuition>

<Concept>
## The one function, precisely

An LLM takes a sequence of **tokens** (chunks of text — the next lesson is all
about them) and outputs a **probability for every token in its vocabulary**
being the next one. After "The capital of France is", it might put 92% on
" Paris", 3% on " the", a sliver on everything else. Generation is just this
function in a loop: pick a next token, append it, run the function again —
one token at a time, each conditioned on everything so far. That loop is
called **autoregressive generation**, and it's why responses stream word by
word: the model literally doesn't know its second sentence until it has
committed to its first.

Two vocabulary words you now own:

- **Parameters (weights):** the billions of learned numbers inside the model.
  "A 70B model" means 70 billion of them. They ARE the model.
- **Context window:** the maximum number of tokens the model can condition on
  at once — its working memory. Nothing outside the window exists, as far as
  the model is concerned.
</Concept>

<Concept>
## Training vs inference — two different lives

A model lives twice, and confusing the two lives causes most misconceptions:

**Training** happens once, before you ever meet the model. The weights start
random; the model is shown trillions of tokens of text and, for each one,
asked to predict the next. Every miss nudges the weights. Months of compute
later, the weights encode an absurdly good statistical portrait of language —
and of the facts, styles, and reasoning patterns embedded in it. Then the
weights are **frozen**.

**Inference** is every conversation after that. Your prompt runs through the
frozen weights; nothing you say changes them. The model doesn't remember
yesterday's chat, doesn't learn your name between sessions, and knows nothing
after its **knowledge cutoff** — the date its training data ended. When a
chat app "remembers," that's the *application* stuffing history back into the
context window, not the model learning.
</Concept>

<RealWorld source="Autocomplete on steroids">
Your phone keyboard predicts the next word from the last few. An LLM is the
same *shape* of machine scaled beyond recognition: instead of two words of
context, up to hundreds of thousands of tokens; instead of frequency tables,
billions of weights arranged in a transformer (Module 3). The punchline is
that at this scale, "predict the next token" stops looking like autocomplete —
to guess the next token of a half-written proof or program, the model is
forced to internalize a working model of proofs and programs. Capability
emerges from prediction. Keep both halves: it is *just* autocomplete, and
that's exactly why it's so much more.
</RealWorld>

<Concept>
## Base models vs the assistant you actually talk to

Pretraining alone produces a **base model** — a pure text-continuer. Ask it
"What's the capital of France?" and it might reply with *another geography
question*, because quiz lists in its training data look exactly like that.
Continuation, not conversation.

Turning that into an assistant takes a second, much smaller phase:
**instruction tuning** (fine-tuning on many example instruction→good-response
pairs) and **preference alignment** — RLHF and friends — where the model is
optimized toward responses humans rate as helpful, honest, and safe. Module 11
goes deeper; for now the takeaway is that "chatting" is a *learned behavior
on top of* next-token prediction, not the native mode. The chat format itself
(system prompt, user turns, assistant turns) is just structured text laid into
the context window — which is why Module 5's prompting techniques work at all.
</Concept>

<Gotcha>
The model is **not a database**, and its confident tone is not a confidence
score. It produces the most *plausible-sounding* continuation — plausibility
learned from text, not truth checked against a source. So it will state a
wrong fact in the same fluent register as a right one, invent citations that
"look like" citations, and never say "I don't know" unless trained or prompted
to. This one gotcha predicts the shape of the whole field: RAG exists to bolt
sources on (Module 6), tool use to fetch ground truth (Module 7), and
evaluation to measure the damage (Module 10).
</Gotcha>

<Aside title="Why bigger got better — scaling, in one paragraph">
The empirical surprise of the last decade: make the model bigger, feed it more
text, spend more compute, and next-token prediction improves *smoothly and
predictably* — and qualitatively new abilities (translation, coding,
multi-step reasoning) show up along the way without being explicitly taught.
These "scaling laws" are why labs race to train ever-larger models, and why
model families come in sizes (a phone-sized 3B, a datacenter 400B): same
recipe, different points on the curve, with cost and capability rising
together. Module 12 picks up the cost half of that story.
</Aside>

<Cheatsheet>
The sentence to carry into every other lesson: **an LLM is frozen weights that
turn a context window of tokens into a probability distribution over the next
token — trained once on the internet, remembering nothing, checking nothing.**
When behavior surprises you, ask which part of that sentence explains it;
one of them always does.
</Cheatsheet>
