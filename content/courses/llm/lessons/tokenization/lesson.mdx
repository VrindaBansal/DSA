<Intuition>
Before a language model can be clever, it has to *read* — and it does not read
the way you do. It cannot see letters. The very first thing that happens to
your prompt is that it gets chopped into **tokens** — sub-word chunks — and
turned into a list of integers. Everything the model knows, reasons about, and
bills you for is measured in these tokens. Most of the model's weirdest
behaviors — miscounting letters, choking on arithmetic, costing 3× more for
Japanese than English — are not reasoning failures. They are shadows cast by
this first, invisible step. Understand tokenization and a whole category of
"why did it do that?" dissolves.
</Intuition>

<Concept>
## A token is not a word

A token is a chunk of bytes that the model treats as one atomic unit. It's
usually a piece of a word: common words like `" the"` are a single token,
while a rarer word like `"tokenization"` might split into `token` + `ization`.
The model has a fixed **vocabulary** — typically 50,000 to 200,000 tokens —
and every input is expressed as a sequence of IDs into that vocabulary.

Three consequences follow immediately, and they explain a lot:

- The model's atomic unit is the token, so it has no *direct* access to the
  letters inside a token. "How many r's in strawberry?" asks about something
  below its resolution.
- Cost and context limits are counted in tokens, not characters or words.
- The *same* string always tokenizes the same way (given a vocabulary) — the
  splitting is deterministic, not a model decision.
</Concept>

<RealWorld source="The strawberry problem">
The famous "how many r's are in strawberry?" failure isn't stupidity. To the
model, `strawberry` may arrive as three tokens — something like
`st` · `raw` · `berry`. The letters are fused inside those chunks; the model
has to *reconstruct* the spelling from memory of how the word looks, then
count — a genuinely awkward task for a system whose inputs are token IDs.
The same root cause makes models unreliable at reversing strings, counting
characters, and doing digit-by-digit arithmetic: all of these need
character-level vision the tokenizer took away. It's why tool use (a
calculator, a `len()` call) beats asking the model to do it in its head.
</RealWorld>

<Concept>
## How the vocabulary gets built: byte-pair encoding

Where do the tokens come from? The dominant algorithm is **byte-pair encoding
(BPE)**. It's greedy and beautifully simple:

1. Start with the smallest possible units — individual bytes/characters — so
   *any* text is representable (no "unknown token" holes).
2. Scan a huge corpus and find the **most frequent adjacent pair** of units.
3. **Merge** that pair into a new single token; add it to the vocabulary.
4. Repeat thousands of times.

Frequent sequences — `t`+`h` → `th`, then `th`+`e` → `the` — get merged early
and become single tokens. Rare sequences never merge and stay in pieces. The
result: common words are one token, rare words are several, and the vocabulary
naturally allocates its "resolution" where the language actually spends its
mass. Drive the merge process yourself below and watch a vocabulary assemble
from raw characters.
</Concept>

<Concept>
## Why token counts explode (and your bill with them)

Because BPE learns its merges from a (mostly English) training corpus, the
"~4 characters per token" rule of thumb only holds for ordinary English prose.
Everything else pays a tax:

- **Code** is punctuation-dense and full of rare identifiers, so it fragments
  into many tokens.
- **Non-English / non-Latin scripts** whose byte sequences the tokenizer rarely
  merged can hit 1 character ≈ 1+ tokens — a 2–3× penalty versus English for
  the same *meaning*.
- **Numbers** tokenize erratically (`1000` vs `1 000` vs `1,000` split
  differently), which is one reason raw arithmetic is unreliable.

The context window — "128k tokens" — is a *token* budget, so the same wall
holds less Japanese text than English text. When you estimate cost or "will
this fit," count tokens, not characters.
</Concept>

<Gotcha>
Whitespace and capitalization change token IDs. `" dog"` (with a leading
space) and `"dog"` are **different tokens**, and `"Dog"` is different again.
This is why a stray leading space or trailing newline in a prompt template can
subtly shift behavior, and why few-shot examples should match the exact
spacing you'll use at inference. The model is reacting to token IDs, and those
IDs are whitespace- and case-sensitive.
</Gotcha>

<Aside title="Why not just use characters as tokens? (or whole words?)">
Two failed extremes explain why BPE won. **Character tokens** make every
sequence enormous — attention is quadratic in sequence length (Module 3), so
character-level models are brutally expensive for the same text. **Word
tokens** can't handle words they never saw (names, typos, new slang, code) —
you get "unknown token" holes and a vocabulary that must be impossibly large
to cover everything. BPE is the pragmatic middle: start from bytes so
*nothing* is unrepresentable, then merge up to word-ish chunks so common text
stays short. Subword tokenization is the compromise that made large
vocabularies and long context affordable at once.
</Aside>

<Concept>
## Build it: one BPE merge step

The whole algorithm is "find the most frequent adjacent pair, merge it,
repeat." Implement a single merge step — given a sequence of tokens and the
pair to merge, produce the new sequence — and you understand BPE. The tests
check that every occurrence of the pair is fused and everything else is left
alone.
</Concept>

<Cheatsheet>
The one line to keep: **the model reads token IDs, not letters — so cost,
context limits, and a whole family of spelling/counting/math failures are
tokenization artifacts, not reasoning ones.** When something weird happens
with characters or numbers, suspect the tokenizer first, and reach for a tool.
</Cheatsheet>
