<Intuition>
A base model knows only what was in its training data, frozen at a point in
time, with no citations and no access to your private files. RAG —
retrieval-augmented generation — is the fix, and the idea is almost
insultingly simple: **before you ask the model to answer, go find the relevant
text and paste it into the prompt.** The model then answers *from* that text
instead of from memory. That one move buys you current information, private
knowledge, and citations — the three things a raw model can't give you. The
entire craft of RAG is making the "go find the relevant text" step actually
work, because when RAG fails, it almost always fails there.
</Intuition>

<Concept>
## The pipeline, stage by stage

RAG is a small assembly line, and each station has its own way of breaking:

1. **Chunk** — split your documents into passages. Too big and a chunk mixes
   topics (diluting relevance); too small and it loses the context that makes
   it answerable.
2. **Embed** — turn each chunk into a vector (Module 2) and store it in a
   vector database.
3. **Retrieve** — embed the user's query, find the nearest chunks by cosine
   similarity. *This is where most RAG bugs live.*
4. **Rerank** — optionally re-score the top-N candidates with a stronger
   cross-encoder model that reads query and chunk together. A big precision
   win.
5. **Generate** — stuff the surviving chunks into the prompt and ask the model
   to answer, grounded in them.

The load-bearing insight: **RAG is retrieval first, generation second.** If
the answer-bearing chunk never makes it into step 5, no prompt engineering can
save you. Watch the whole pipeline run below, then we'll pull apart the two
halves.
</Concept>

<RealWorld source="Docs Q&A">
The canonical RAG app is "chat with your docs": a support bot over a product
manual, a legal assistant over contracts, this very portal's tutor over its
own lessons. When it answers "I couldn't find that" for something that's
plainly in the manual, the instinct is to blame the model — but the log almost
always shows the right passage was never retrieved. The chunk was split
badly, or the user phrased it in words the embedding didn't match, or ten
mediocre chunks crowded out the one good one. Fixing the generation prompt
does nothing; fixing retrieval fixes the bug. This is the single most
important reflex in production RAG.
</RealWorld>

## The three highest-leverage knobs

When retrieval underperforms, reach for these in order:

- **Chunking.** The highest-leverage and most-neglected knob. Respect
  structure (headings, paragraphs), keep chunks self-contained, and use modest
  overlap so a sentence split across a boundary isn't lost. Bad chunking caps
  the quality of everything downstream.
- **Hybrid search.** Pure vector search is semantic — great for "how do I
  cancel" matching "unsubscribe," but it *misses exact tokens*: SKUs, error
  codes, function names, people's names. Combine it with lexical search
  (BM25) so exact identifiers still hit. Hybrid catches what each half misses.
- **Reranking.** Vector top-k is fast but coarse. A cross-encoder reranker
  reads the query and each candidate *together* and re-scores — turning "top
  20 roughly-relevant" into "top 3 actually-relevant." It's often the biggest
  single-step precision jump.
</Concept>

<Aside title="Why not skip retrieval and cross-encode everything?">
It sounds like the cross-encoder should just replace the whole first stage — if
reading query and chunk *together* is more accurate, why bother with the vector
search at all? Cost. The initial retrieval uses a **bi-encoder**: it embeds the
query and every chunk *separately*, so all your document vectors can be computed
once, ahead of time, and searched in milliseconds even across millions of
chunks. A **cross-encoder** never precomputes anything — it must run a fresh
forward pass on the query-and-chunk pair for *every* candidate, so scoring a
million chunks per query is hopeless. That's the whole reason RAG is two stages:
the cheap bi-encoder casts a wide net to ~20 candidates, then the accurate
cross-encoder does the expensive close read on just those. Fast-and-approximate
narrows; slow-and-precise refines.
</Aside>

<Concept>
## Lost in the middle, and grounding

Two more effects separate working RAG from a demo:

**Lost in the middle.** Models attend most reliably to the *start* and *end*
of their context and least to the middle. So stuffing in more chunks can
backfire — the answer gets buried in the low-attention middle. Retrieve
*fewer, better* chunks (that's what reranking is for) and place the strongest
ones at the edges.

**Grounding.** Even with the right chunk in context, a model will sometimes
answer from its own memory instead. Instruct it explicitly: *"Answer only using
the provided context. If the answer isn't there, say you don't know. Cite the
source."* Grounding is the main lever that converts RAG from "confident guesses
with extra steps" into "citable, checkable answers."
</Concept>

<Concept>
## When RAG is the wrong tool

RAG is not a hammer for every nail. Skip it when:

- The knowledge is already in-weights and stable (general reasoning, common
  facts) — retrieval just adds latency and failure surface.
- The task is pure transformation (summarize *this* text, rewrite *this*
  email) — there's nothing to retrieve.
- A precise lookup would do — if the answer is "the current balance," call the
  database, don't embed it.

And remember RAG's relationship to fine-tuning: **RAG supplies *what* to say
(facts); fine-tuning shapes *how* to say it (behavior/format).** They're
complementary, not competing.
</Concept>

<Cheatsheet>
Say it like a practitioner: **"RAG is retrieval first — chunk, hybrid-retrieve,
rerank, then ground the generation. When it's wrong, I check whether the right
chunk was even retrieved before I touch the prompt."** That reflex, plus
grounding to allow "I don't know," is 80% of production RAG quality.
</Cheatsheet>
