<Intuition>
If tokenization is how a model *reads*, embeddings are how it (and your search
system) *measures meaning*. An embedding turns a piece of text into a vector —
a point in a few-hundred-dimensional space — arranged so that texts about the
same thing land near each other, regardless of the exact words. "How do I
cancel?" and "unsubscribe me" end up as neighbors. That geometric trick —
meaning as *direction* — is the entire foundation of semantic search, dedup,
clustering, recommendations, and the retrieval half of RAG. Compare two vectors
by the angle between them (cosine similarity) and you have a relevance score
that ignores length and fixates on topic.
</Intuition>

<Concept>
## Where the vector actually comes from

"Turns text into a vector" sounds like magic, so pin down the mechanism. An
**embedding model** — usually a transformer, a cousin of the one in Module 3 —
reads your text and emits a fixed-length list of numbers (say 768 or 1536 of
them). It isn't hand-designed; it's *trained*. During training the model is
shown pairs of texts that belong together (a question and its answer, a
sentence and its paraphrase) and pairs that don't, and its weights are nudged
until "belongs together" pairs land close in the space and unrelated pairs land
far apart. This is called **contrastive training**, and it's the whole reason
the geometry means anything: closeness in the space *was optimized to* mean
closeness in meaning.

Two consequences you'll rely on all course:

- The vector for a piece of text is **deterministic** given the model — same
  text in, same vector out — which is why you can compute a document's vectors
  once and store them.
- The space is **model-specific**. Vectors from two different embedding models
  are not comparable — never mix them in one index (that's the gotcha in the
  cheatsheet).
</Concept>
