<Intuition>
A model doesn't "write an answer" — it predicts a probability distribution over
the *next token*, you pick one, append it, and repeat. Every knob you've heard
of — temperature, top-k, top-p — is just a policy for turning that distribution
into a choice. Temperature 0 always takes the single most likely token
(reproducible, ideal for extraction and tool calls); higher temperature
flattens the distribution so lower-probability tokens get a chance (diversity,
ideal for brainstorming). Understanding decoding also demystifies
hallucination: the sampler has no truth oracle: when the distribution is
confidently wrong, you get fluent, plausible falsehood. That's the mechanism
working as designed, not a bug you can turn off with a lower temperature.
</Intuition>

<Concept>
## From logits to probabilities: the softmax

The model's very last step outputs one raw score per token in its vocabulary —
tens of thousands of numbers called **logits**. A logit isn't a probability;
it's just "how much the model favors this token," and it can be negative or
huge. To turn the whole vector of logits into a probability distribution (all
positive, summing to 1), you run it through the **softmax**: exponentiate every
logit and divide each by the total. Exponentiating makes everything positive and
stretches the gaps, so a token that was a little ahead in logits becomes a lot
ahead in probability; dividing by the sum normalizes it. Softmax is the exact
same function attention uses (Module 3) — worth learning once.

**Temperature** slots in right here: before softmax, every logit is divided by
the temperature T. T = 1 leaves the distribution as-is. T below 1 makes the
logits more spread out, so softmax sharpens toward the top token — at T → 0 it
becomes "always pick the single highest," which is greedy decoding. T above 1
squashes the logits together, flattening the distribution so unlikely tokens get
a real chance. So temperature isn't a creativity dial bolted on the side; it's
one number scaling the logits before the same softmax runs.
</Concept>
