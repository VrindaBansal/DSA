<Intuition>
The transformer's one big idea is **attention**: to build a representation of a
word, look at every other word in the context and take a weighted average of
them, where the weights say "how relevant is this word to that one?" That's
how "it" figures out what it refers to, and how "bank" learns whether it's a
river or a vault — from the company each token keeps. Everything downstream of a
language model is stacks of this operation. Its two most consequential
properties fall straight out of the mechanism: context cost grows with the
*square* of length (every token looks at every token), and generation stays
affordable only because of a cache. Below: attention weights as a live heatmap.
</Intuition>

<Concept>
## What Q, K, and V actually are

You'll see attention written as `softmax(QKᵀ/√d)·V`, and the letters look
arbitrary until you see the analogy: **attention is a soft search**. From each
token the model computes three different vectors, all just learned linear
projections of that token's current representation:

- **Query (Q)** — "what am I looking for?" The word *it* emits a query that
  effectively asks *which earlier noun do I refer to?*
- **Key (K)** — "what do I offer as a match?" Every token advertises a key, like
  the label on a filing cabinet drawer.
- **Value (V)** — "what do I contribute if you attend to me?" The actual
  information a token hands over once it's been matched.

The mechanism is then exactly a lookup made soft: dot each token's **query**
against every **key** (`QKᵀ`) to get a relevance score for every pair, divide by
`√d` to keep the numbers stable, `softmax` the scores into weights that sum to 1
(Module 4's softmax, reused here), and take that weighted average of the
**values**. So "*it* attends to *dog*" literally means *it*'s query matched
*dog*'s key, so *dog*'s value dominates the average. Because every query meets
every key, the score matrix is n×n — and that's the quadratic cost the next
check is about.
</Concept>
